Probability Theory & Statistical Distributions

The Binomial & Normal Distributions — Study Notes

Every statistical test you will ever run — a t-test, a chi-square test, a confidence interval — is built on a foundation of probability. These notes explain that foundation in the plainest possible language, using dice, blood donors and coin-flips to build intuition, then apply it to real peer-reviewed evidence from Zimbabwe and Southern Africa — birth weight, HIV testing accuracy, viral suppression and low birth weight. Every symbol is explained the moment it appears, and every formula is paired with a picture.

Section 01 · Foundations

Why Probability Matters to a Clinician-Researcher

Every time you read “p < 0.05” in a journal article, order a screening test, or counsel a patient on their chance of a treatment working, you are using probability — whether you call it that or not. Probability is simply the mathematics of uncertainty, and clinical medicine is built entirely on uncertainty: no test is perfect, no patient responds exactly like the textbook, and no sample of patients looks exactly like the next sample.

90.0%

Positive-percent agreement (a sensitivity-type measure) of routine rapid HIV testing among women during Zimbabwe’s 2017 national antenatal surveillance — meaning 1 in 10 truly positive results were missed under real clinic conditions (Gregson S et al., J Int AIDS Soc, 2021).

16.7%

Proportion of live births classified as low birth weight (<2.5 kg) among 3,110 deliveries at Harare Maternity Hospital — a single proportion that is really a binomial probability in disguise (Feresu SA et al., PLoS ONE, 2015).

Both numbers describe uncertainty about an individual: will this test correctly detect HIV in this woman? Will this baby be born under 2.5 kg? Probability theory gives us the rules and shapes (“distributions”) that describe how such uncertain, individual events behave predictably in large numbers — which is exactly what makes statistical inference possible in Topic 4.

Section 02 · Foundations

The Language of Probability

Probability has its own small alphabet of symbols. None of them are difficult once you know what each one is asking. Every rule below reads left to right, and every symbol is defined the moment it appears.

A probability is simply a number between 0 and 1 (or 0% and 100%) that tells you how likely something is. 0 means “never happens.” 1 means “always happens.” Everything else sits somewhere in between — exactly like the rainfall forecasts you already read every day: “30% chance of rain over Harare this afternoon” means that, out of every 100 days with this exact weather pattern, rain would fall on roughly 30 of them.

P(not A)“P of not A”The probability A does not happen. Always equals 1 − P(A)P(HIV-negative) = 1 − P(HIV-positive)
P(A or B)“P of A or B”The probability that A happens, B happens, or both happenP(blood group A or group O)

One rule that never changes: a probability can never be negative, and it can never be bigger than 1 (100%). If your calculation gives you 1.4 or −0.2, you have made an arithmetic mistake — go back and check it.

Section 03 · Foundations

Two Basic Rules of Probability

Almost every probability calculation you will ever do in clinical research boils down to one of two simple questions: am I asking about “this OR that”, or am I asking about “this AND that”? Each question has its own rule.

Rule 1 — The Addition (“OR”) Rule

Use this when two events cannot happen at the same time — epidemiologists call these mutually exclusive events. A person cannot be blood group A and blood group O at the same time; you simply add the two probabilities.

P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B)
“the chance of either happening” = “chance of the first” + “chance of the second” — only valid when A and B cannot both occur
Mutually exclusive events
The two circles never touch — nobody belongs to both groups at once.

Worked example (illustrative blood-bank figures): Suppose roughly 46% of donors at a Southern African blood bank are group O and 30% are group A. What is the probability the next donor is group A or group O?

P(A or O) = 0.30 + 0.46 = 0.76 = 76%
Roughly 3 in 4 donors will be group A or group O.

Rule 2 — The Multiplication (“AND”) Rule

Use this when two events are independent — the outcome of one has no effect whatsoever on the outcome of the other. Whether one unrelated patient is virally suppressed tells you nothing about whether a second, unrelated patient is. When events are independent, you multiply the probabilities.

P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)
“the chance of both happening” = “chance of the first” × “chance of the second” — only valid when A and B are independent

Worked example, using real regional data: Musomekwa & van Wyk (2025) found that 68.6% of 315 patients on second-line ART in Bulawayo were virally suppressed. If we select two unrelated patients at random from this population, what is the probability that both are virally suppressed?

Patient 1 suppressed
→
P = 0.686
Patient 2 suppressed
→
P = 0.686
Both suppressed (AND)
→
0.686 × 0.686 = 0.47 (47%)

Notice that AND makes probabilities smaller (it is harder for two things to both happen), while OR makes probabilities larger (it is easier for at least one thing to happen).

Section 04 · Applied Probability

Probability in Diagnostic Testing

Every time you order a test, you are asking a conditional-probability question: P(A|B), “the probability of A, given that B is true.” A diagnostic test is simply a tool for estimating one conditional probability (does this person have the disease?) using a different, measurable conditional probability (how often does the test get it right?).

Two conditional probabilities every test has

TermIn symbolsPlain-language question
SensitivityP(test + | truly has disease)Of everyone who truly has the disease, what fraction does the test correctly catch?
SpecificityP(test − | truly disease-free)Of everyone who is truly disease-free, what fraction does the test correctly clear?

Notice the two questions run in opposite directions to the question a patient actually asks: “I tested positive — what is the probability I actually have the disease?” That is P(disease | test+), the mirror image of sensitivity, and it is called the positive predictive value. Confusing the two directions is one of the most common statistical errors in clinical medicine (see Pitfall 3, Section 10).

RAPID HIV TEST ACCURACY IN ROUTINE USE — ZIMBABWE NATIONAL ANTENATAL SURVEILLANCE, 2017

Gregson S, Moorhouse L, Dadirai T, Sheppard H, Mayini J, Beckmann N, Skovdal M, Dzangare J, Moyo B, Maswera R, Pinsky BA, Mharakurwa S, Francis I, Mugurungi O, Wandeler G, Nyamukapa C investigated rapid diagnostic test (RDT) performance at 62 antenatal sites during Zimbabwe’s 2017 national HIV surveillance round, comparing routine RDT results against a laboratory-confirmed reference standard.

Positive-percent agreement (sensitivity-type measure) among women = 90.0%
Negative-percent agreement (specificity-type measure) = 99.9%

In plain language: of every 100 pregnant women who were truly HIV-positive, the routine rapid test correctly identified about 90 — but missed roughly 10 (false negatives), largely attributable to storeroom humidity and temperature exceeding manufacturer specifications, and RDT results being read before the recommended waiting time. False-positive results were comparatively rare (specificity 99.9%), but the authors note that historical misclassification may have already placed some uninfected individuals on lifelong ART.

Why this matters for probability: sensitivity and specificity are properties of the test and stay roughly constant wherever it is used. But the number a worried patient actually wants — “given my positive result, what is my real chance of having HIV?” — also depends on how common HIV already is in that clinic's population. The same test result carries different weight in a high-prevalence antenatal clinic than in a low-prevalence rural outreach programme.

Gregson S et al. J Int AIDS Soc. 2021;24(4):e25700. doi:10.1002/jia2.25700.

Section 05 · Building Blocks

Random Variables & Probability Distributions

A random variable is just a quantity attached to an uncertain event — a number whose exact value you cannot know in advance, but whose possible values and how likely each one is can be described mathematically. A probability distribution is that description: a table, list or curve that shows every possible value of the random variable next to how likely it is — the mathematical equivalent of a full week's rainfall forecast rather than just tomorrow's single percentage.

1

Discrete random variables

Take only whole-number, countable values — you are counting how many times something happens. There is nothing “in between” the values.

ExampleThe number of babies with low birth weight among the next 10 born at a clinic: 0, 1, 2 … 10. Never 3.5.
DistributionThe Binomial distribution (Section 06)
2

Continuous random variables

Can take any value within a range — you are measuring something, not counting it. There are infinitely many possible values between any two points.

ExampleA newborn's birth weight: 2.94 kg, 3.107 kg, 3.1072 kg … as precisely as your scale can measure.
DistributionThe Normal distribution (Section 07)

Ask yourself one question to tell them apart: “Am I counting occurrences, or am I measuring an amount?” Counting → binomial family. Measuring → normal family (or another continuous shape). This one question will guide almost every choice of statistical test in Topic 5.

Section 06 · Discrete Distributions

The Binomial Distribution

The binomial distribution answers one very common clinical-research question: “If I repeat the same yes/no experiment n times, each time with the same chance p of a ‘yes’, what is the probability of getting exactly k ‘yeses’?”

The four conditions that must hold

  • A fixed number of trials, n (e.g. 10 births, 315 patients).
  • Each trial has only two possible outcomes — “success” or “failure” (LBW or not; suppressed or not).
  • The probability of success, p, is the same on every trial.
  • The trials are independent — one outcome does not influence another.
P(X = k) = ⁿCk × pk × (1−p)(n−k)
“the chance of exactly k successes” = “number of ways to arrange k successes among n trials” × “chance of that many successes” × “chance of the rest being failures”
SymbolWhat it means here
nNumber of independent trials (patients, births, tests)
pProbability of “success” on one trial (same every time)
kThe exact number of successes we want the probability of
ⁿCk“n choose k” — the number of different ways k successes can be arranged among n trials
Mean = n × p      SD = √[ n × p × (1−p) ]
On average you expect n×p successes; the typical spread around that average is given by the square-root formula.

Picturing the shape: 10 births, p = 16.7% low birth weight

Using the Feresu et al. (2015) Harare figure of 16.7% low birth weight as p, here is the probability of seeing exactly k low-birth-weight babies among the next 10 births at a similar facility (illustrative, rounded calculation from the binomial formula):

Most likely outcome: 1–2 low-birth-weight babies out of the next 10 (mean = n×p = 10 × 0.167 ≈ 1.7). Bars beyond k=5 are vanishingly small and omitted.

Regional evidence: two binomial scenarios

LOW BIRTH WEIGHT — HARARE MATERNITY HOSPITAL (FERESU ET AL., 2015)

Feresu SA, Harlow SD, Woelk GB analysed 3,110 deliveries at Harare Maternity Hospital, Zimbabwe. The frequency of low birth weight (<2.5 kg) was 16.7%.

Each delivery: two outcomes (LBW or not), roughly constant p ≈ 0.167 → total LBW count across n=3,110 approximates Binomial(n=3110, p=0.167)

Caveat: strict independence requires that one birth's outcome does not influence another's. In reality, LBW clusters within mothers who deliver more than once, and within shared risk factors (malaria, malnutrition, lack of antenatal care) across a community — a reminder that the binomial model is a simplification, addressed further in Section 10.

Feresu SA et al. PLoS ONE. 2015;10(6):e0129705. doi:10.1371/journal.pone.0129705.

VIRAL SUPPRESSION ON SECOND-LINE ART — BULAWAYO (MUSOMEKWA & VAN WYK, 2025)

Among 315 participants on second-line ART for at least 6 months in Bulawayo, 68.6% achieved viral suppression.

n = 315, p = 0.686 → expected number suppressed = n×p = 216.2 ≈ the 216 patients actually observed as suppressed

This shows the binomial mean formula (n×p) working exactly as predicted against real clinic data — the theoretical expectation and the observed count match almost precisely.

Musomekwa K, van Wyk B. Int J Environ Res Public Health. 2025;22(5):730. doi:10.3390/ijerph22050730.

Section 07 · Continuous Distributions

The Normal Distribution

The normal distribution is the familiar symmetric “bell curve.” Most naturally occurring measurements — height, birth weight, blood pressure in a healthy population — cluster around a central average, with progressively fewer people as you move further away from that average in either direction. Two numbers completely describe a normal curve: the mean (μ), which sets its centre, and the standard deviation (σ), which sets how wide or narrow it is.

68% within ±1σ 95% within ±2σ 99.7% within ±3σ

The 68–95–99.7 rule (the “Empirical Rule”)

For any approximately normal measurement: about 68% of values fall within one standard deviation of the mean; about 95% fall within two; and about 99.7% — almost everybody — falls within three. A value more than 3σ from the mean is genuinely unusual.

Regional evidence: birth weight as a bell curve

ZIMBABWEAN BIRTHWEIGHT-FOR-GESTATION STANDARDS (MUNJANJA & MASONA, 1990)

Munjanja SP, Masona D derived birthweight-for-gestation standards from 5,872 women with confirmed menstrual dates delivering in Harare, Zimbabwe, smoothing the 5th, 50th and 95th centiles of birth weight from 24 to 42 weeks’ gestation. Near term, birth weight clusters symmetrically around the 50th centile — the classic signature of an approximately normal distribution.

For teaching purposes: assume μ ≈ 3.1 kg and σ ≈ 0.45 kg for term birth weight (rounded, illustrative values typical of Southern African birth-weight studies, not exact figures from the original centile tables)

Using these illustrative values, the WHO low-birth-weight cutoff of 2.5 kg sits at z = (2.5−3.1)/0.45 = −1.33, which a standard normal table places at roughly the lowest 9% of a perfectly normal curve.

Compare this theoretical ~9% to the 16.7% actually observed by Feresu et al. (2015) at a different Harare facility. The gap is a valuable teaching point: real biological distributions are never perfectly normal — birth weight has a longer, heavier lower tail (driven by prematurity, malaria, hypertensive disease and undernutrition) than a symmetric bell curve predicts. The normal distribution is a useful approximation, not an exact law of nature.

Munjanja SP, Masona D. Cent Afr J Med. 1990;36(6):144–147. PMID: 2261629.

Section 08 · Continuous Distributions

Standardising with Z-scores

A z-score converts any value from any normal distribution onto one universal scale — “how many standard deviations above or below average.” This lets you compare a birth weight in kilograms with a blood pressure in mmHg on the very same yardstick.

z = (x − μ) / σ
“your value, minus the average, divided by the typical spread”
SymbolMeaning
xThe individual value you are standardising (e.g. one baby's birth weight)
μThe population mean
σThe population standard deviation
zThe result — positive if above average, negative if below
1

Worked example — birth weight

A baby weighs 2.5 kg. Using μ=3.1 kg, σ=0.45 kg (Section 07):

z = (2.5−3.1) / 0.45 = −1.33

A negative z-score of 1.33 means the baby weighs 1.33 SDs below the average — light, but not extreme (well within ±2σ, the “95%” band).

2

Worked example — blood pressure (illustrative clinic data)

A Harare primary-care clinic's systolic BP readings for one morning have μ=128 mmHg, σ=15 mmHg. A patient reads 158 mmHg:

z = (158−128) / 15 = +2.00

z=+2 sits right at the edge of the 95% band (±2σ) — among the highest ~2.5% of readings that morning, worth a repeat measurement and clinical review.

The blood-pressure figures above are illustrative teaching data, not drawn from a specific published study; hypertension screening thresholds and prevalence in young Zimbabwean adults are documented in Sabapathy K et al. (2024), listed in the references.

Section 10 · Reading Critically

Common Pitfalls

Each concept above has a characteristic way it gets misused in practice.

Assuming independence that doesn't hold

The binomial model needs independent trials. Repeat pregnancies from the same mother, or babies born in the same clinic sharing the same risk exposures (malaria season, staffing shortages), violate this — as flagged for the Feresu et al. low-birth-weight data in Section 06.

Treating a skewed variable as normal

Viral load and CD4 count are strongly right-skewed (a long tail of very high or very low values), not symmetric bell curves. Applying z-scores or the 68–95–99.7 rule directly to raw viral load is a common but incorrect analysis — a log-transformation is usually needed first.

Confusing sensitivity with predictive value

P(test+|disease) [sensitivity] and P(disease|test+) [predictive value] are different conditional probabilities running in opposite directions. As Section 04 showed, a test's sensitivity and specificity stay fairly constant, but its predictive value shifts with local disease prevalence.

Mistaking an unusual result for an impossible one

A z-score beyond ±2 is uncommon (about 1 in 20), not impossible. With enough patients, tests or clinics, rare combinations will occur by chance alone — this is exactly why hypothesis testing (Topic 4) sets a formal threshold rather than relying on a single surprising number.

Your weekly quiz and Final Presentation Project will ask you to identify which rule or distribution applies to a scenario, and to spot which of these four pitfalls a published or hypothetical analysis has fallen into.
