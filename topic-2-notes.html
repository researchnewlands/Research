Section 01 · Foundations

Why "Who Counts?" Is the First Question

The basis of all epidemiological research is a measure of the occurrence of health-related states or events — "cases" — in specified populations. Before any number can be calculated, two things must be pinned down: what counts as a case, and which population those cases were drawn from. Get either wrong, and every rate, ratio or comparison built on top of it is unreliable.

110 vs 88

Per 100,000 women: two populations' reported breast cancer rates in the same year. Without knowing whether both used the same case definition, this difference cannot be interpreted.

34,549

Suspected cholera cases recorded in Zimbabwe's 2023–24 outbreak — a figure that depends entirely on where the surveillance case definition draws its line (Olatunji et al., J Epidemiol Glob Health, 2024).

Traditionally, epidemiological research focused on the occurrence of disease. More recently, epidemiologists have studied a much wider range of health-related conditions — both negative and positive — including risk factors, behaviours and physiological states. What unites all of this work is the same opening move: define the case, then define the population.

Section 02 · The Founding Case

John Snow and the Broad Street Pump, 1854

Long before laboratory confirmation of Vibrio cholerae was possible, John Snow demonstrated that disciplined case definition and population thinking could solve a public health emergency. His investigation remains the clearest illustration of this topic's two central ideas.

1853
Recurring London cholera epidemics; miasma theory dominant
SEP 1854
Soho outbreak: >500 deaths within 10 days near Broad Street
7 SEP
Snow persuades the parish to remove the pump handle
AFTER
New cases subside; later confirmed by Whitehead's independent survey
CASE

How Snow defined a case

Snow could not test for the organism — Robert Koch would not identify Vibrio cholerae until decades later. Instead he used a clinical and circumstantial case definition: a death attributed to cholera, plotted by the exact address where the person lived or died, during the defined outbreak period of August–September 1854.

A case definition does not require a laboratory test — it requires a consistent, applicable rule.
POP

How Snow defined the population

Snow's population at risk was everyone who drew water from a given pump or water company — not "the population of London". He located roughly 700 deaths within a 250-yard radius and compared households served by the Southwark & Vauxhall Company against those served by the Lambeth Company, whose intake had moved upstream of the sewage outflow.

Choosing the right denominator is what let a count of deaths become a rate that could be compared.
THE "GRAND EXPERIMENT", LONDON, 1854

Snow identified that two water companies supplied overlapping streets in South London, with households essentially randomly served by one or the other — a natural experiment. Over a seven-week period, mortality in homes supplied by the sewage-contaminated Southwark & Vauxhall Company was roughly fourteen times higher than in homes supplied by the Lambeth Company. This is widely regarded as one of the first quantitative comparisons between an exposed and an unexposed population in epidemiological history.

Why this matters for Topic 2: Snow could only make this comparison because he had defined his case (cholera death), his exposed and unexposed groups (water company), and his population at risk (households served by each company) with enough precision to count and compare them.

170 YEARS LATER — THE SAME LOGIC, HARARE

In 2008–09, Zimbabwe experienced a cholera epidemic of similar logic to Soho's, but at national reach: 98,592 reported cases and 4,288 deaths over eleven months, with Harare itself a major focus. Luque-Fernández and colleagues (2011, Trans R Soc Trop Med Hyg) applied Snow's exact approach at suburb level, treating each Harare suburb as a defined population at risk and calculating its own cholera attack rate — from 1.2 per 1,000 in Tynwald to 90.3 per 1,000 in Hopley — before comparing them geographically to identify high-risk areas such as Mbare and the south-western suburbs. Like Snow, they could only draw this conclusion because cases and the population at risk in each suburb had been defined consistently enough to compare.

Section 03 · Foundations

Two Things to Define Before You Count Anything

Every epidemiological measurement rests on the same two prior decisions.

1

What we mean by a case

A standardised rule that lets anyone, anywhere, decide whether a given person or event would be counted as a case or not.

This determines the numerator.
2

The population the cases came from

The group of people who could potentially have become — or could become — cases under that same definition.

This determines the denominator.

Numerator and denominator must be built from the same logic. A rate calculated from a carefully defined numerator divided by a poorly defined denominator (or vice versa) is not a meaningful rate — it is two numbers that happen to have been divided.

Section 04 · Defining a Case

The Case Definition

A case definition is a set of standardised criteria used to identify cases. The epidemiological definition of a case is not necessarily the same as the everyday clinical one — a clinician treating an individual patient can use judgement and incomplete information in a way that a surveillance system, comparing thousands of patients across sites, cannot.

Newlands Clinic example — hypertension

Hypertension was defined as a documented systolic blood pressure >140 mmHg and/or diastolic blood pressure >90 mmHg recorded on at least two different days, or current use of antihypertensive medication (Shamu et al., Newlands Clinic cohort). This rule can be applied consistently to every patient record, regardless of who is reviewing the chart.

Zimbabwe example — cholera, 2023–24

Zimbabwe's national cholera surveillance distinguished a suspected case (any person presenting with acute watery diarrhoea, in line with WHO/GTFCC clinical criteria) from a confirmed case (laboratory isolation of Vibrio cholerae O1/O139, or a positive rapid diagnostic test). The final tally — 34,549 suspected cases against a much smaller confirmed subset, with 718 deaths (case-fatality ratio 2.1%) — depends entirely on which of these two case definitions is being reported (Olatunji et al., 2024, J Epidemiol Glob Health; G.A.A.C., 2024).

CASE DEFINITION IN PRACTICE — NATIONAL CHOLERA SURVEILLANCE

During an active outbreak, laboratory confirmation of every case is neither feasible nor necessary for an effective response — most patients are managed clinically on the suspected-case definition alone, exactly as Snow worked from clinical and circumstantial evidence in 1854. Confirmed cases serve a different purpose: verifying that the outbreak is genuinely cholera and tracking strain characteristics such as antimicrobial resistance.

Practical consequence: a news report citing "34,549 cases" and a laboratory report citing "the confirmed cases" are not describing the same numerator — and should never be substituted for one another without saying so.

Why does the case definition matter so much?

Too broad

Over-counts cases and inflates prevalence or incidence estimates.

Too narrow

Misses genuine cases and underestimates the true burden of disease.

Inconsistent

Prevents valid comparison across studies, sites, or time periods.

Undocumented

Even a reasonable definition is unusable by others if it is never written down.

Section 05 · Defining a Case

Three Things a Good Case Definition Should State

A good case definition should let anyone clearly decide whether a person or event counts. To do that, it usually needs to specify three things.

1

Method of identification

How will a case be detected? Clinical symptoms, a laboratory or radiological test, a self-reported questionnaire, or a combination of these. Snow's method was clinical and circumstantial; Zimbabwe's confirmed-case definition is laboratory-based.

2

Boundaries of a case

Where does the condition begin and end? Severity thresholds (e.g. systolic BP >140 mmHg), time windows (e.g. within the outbreak period), and explicit exclusions (e.g. excluding women already hypertensive before 20 weeks' gestation in a pre-eclampsia study) all set the boundary.

3

Unit of analysis

Is the case a person, an episode, a household, a suburb, or an event? In a study of recurrent malaria, for instance, the unit of analysis could be the person (ever infected, yes/no) or the episode (each infection counted separately) — these give different answers to "how much malaria is there?" Luque-Fernández's cholera analysis used the suburb as its unit, not the individual.

Worked example — comparing two populations

Consider two populations, X and Y. In population X, 110 per 100,000 women were reported to have developed breast cancer in a given year; in population Y, the figure was 88 per 100,000. Can we conclude breast cancer is more common in X than Y? Not necessarily — there are several ways to define a case of breast cancer (for example, including all malignant forms versus only invasive disease). The two figures are only comparable if both studies used the same case definition, applied to comparably defined populations.

Section 06 · Defining the Population

"Population" Means Something Different Here

Epidemiologists constantly ask one question: what is the denominator? — meaning, what is the population from which the cases arose? This is not the everyday meaning of the word.

EPI

Epidemiology

Population = the collection of units from which a sample or set of cases is drawn. It can be people, clinics, households, or events, and it is defined by the research question — not by geography.

DEMO

Demography

Population = the inhabitants of a country, district, or area. This is a fixed geographic and administrative concept, used for census and planning purposes.

Newlands Clinic example

The IeDEA–Newlands Clinic cohort enrolled all patients aged ≥16 commencing antiretroviral therapy at Newlands Clinic, Harare, between 2004 and 2015 (Shamu et al., 2017, PLoS ONE). That patient list is the epidemiological population for this cohort — not "all Zimbabweans" and not "all people living with HIV in Africa". The denominator is precisely defined by enrolment criteria, not by a map.

Zimbabwe example — cholera surveillance

When Zimbabwe's Ministry of Health and Child Care reports a national attack rate for the 2023–24 cholera outbreak, the denominator used is the demographic population of Zimbabwe (or of each affected district). But when Luque-Fernández and colleagues calculated suburb-specific attack rates for 2008–09 Harare, their denominator was the demographic population of each individual suburb — a much finer-grained population definition, chosen because it matched the level at which they wanted to detect risk.

The lesson: the same outbreak can be described using several different, equally valid population definitions. What matters is that the population is stated explicitly, and that it is appropriate to the question being asked.

Section 07 · Defining the Population

The Population at Risk

The study population is often called the population at risk, because it represents everyone who could potentially become — or could have become — a case under the case definition being used. How a case is defined directly shapes who belongs in this population.

Worked example — post-partum haemorrhage

In a study of primary post-partum haemorrhage at a hospital, a case was defined as loss of ≥500 mL of blood in the first 24 hours after giving birth. The population at risk was therefore all women who gave birth at the hospital during the study period — and each woman was only "at risk" for the first 24 hours following her own delivery, not for the whole study period.

Worked example — cervical cancer

Women who have had a hysterectomy are not at risk of developing cervical cancer. In principle, the population at risk should exclude them. In practice, routine national statistics on cervical cancer in many countries report new cases as a proportion of all resident women, because hysterectomy status is not recorded in the data source. Reporting this limitation honestly — rather than silently treating the total population as the population at risk — is what keeps the resulting rate interpretable.

APPLYING THIS TO CHOLERA

Strictly, the population at risk of cholera in a given suburb is everyone exposed to the contaminated water source or sanitation failure driving transmission — not simply everyone who lives there. In practice, exposure status at the individual level is rarely known during an active outbreak, so surveillance systems use the resident population of the affected area as a practical (if imperfect) proxy for the population at risk, exactly as national cervical cancer statistics use the total resident population as a proxy when hysterectomy status is unavailable. The key discipline is the same one Snow exercised: be explicit about who is included and excluded, so that others can interpret the resulting rate correctly.

In practice, it can be difficult to exclude people who are not truly at risk of becoming cases from a study population. What matters most is being explicit about who has been included and excluded, so that the resulting rate remains interpretable to others.

Section 08 · Describing a Health Event

Person, Place and Time

Once a case and a population are defined, every health event can — and should — be characterised in three dimensions. Snow's map was, in essence, the first systematic person-place-time description in the field's history.

WHO

Person

Who is affected? Age, sex, occupation, HIV status, ART regimen, BMI, or — for Snow — which households drew water from which pump.

WHERE

Place

Where does it occur? Country, province, district, clinic catchment area, urban vs rural, or — for Snow — the exact street address relative to the Broad Street pump.

WHEN

Time

When does it occur? Calendar period, secular trend, seasonal pattern, or — for Snow — the days before and after the pump handle was removed on 7 September 1854.

Practice identifying person, place and time below.

EXAMPLE STATEMENT — NEWLANDS CLINIC

"The prevalence of hypertension among HIV-positive adults aged ≥50 (65% women) receiving care at Newlands Clinic, Harare, Zimbabwe, was 41.2% as of 1 October 2019."

Person: HIV-positive adults aged ≥50, predominantly women · Place: Newlands Clinic, Harare · Time: October 2019.

A second example, from the founding case: "Cholera deaths clustered among households drawing water from the Broad Street pump, in the Soho district of London, during late August and early September 1854." Person: households using a specific water source. Place: Broad Street, Soho. Time: August–September 1854.

Section 09 · The Focus in Practice

Exposures and Outcomes

Every epidemiological study measures, at minimum, an exposure and an outcome — and the case definition determines exactly how the outcome is counted.

EXP

Exposure (risk factor / determinant)

Any factor that may influence the outcome. Can be biological, behavioural, environmental, or structural — for Snow, which water company supplied a household.

OUT

Outcome

The disease, event, or health-related state of interest — defined by the case definition. It need not be a disease: it could be viral suppression, BMI, or blood-pressure control.

Note: the same variable can be an exposure in one study and an outcome in another — and a case definition only applies to the outcome, never to the exposure.

Section 10 · Reading Critically

Pitfalls When Cases and Populations Are Defined Poorly

Most apparent disagreements between studies — or between a headline figure and a clinical impression — trace back to a mismatch in how cases or populations were defined.

Numerator/denominator mismatch

Using a case definition from one source against a population denominator defined a different way — for example, comparing confirmed cholera cases against the total resident population rather than against tested individuals.

Definition drift over time

A surveillance case definition that is tightened or loosened partway through an outbreak can make case counts rise or fall for reasons that have nothing to do with disease transmission.

Conflating prevalence concepts

Reporting "cases" without specifying whether the unit of analysis is people or episodes — important for any condition, like malaria or hypertension flares, that can recur in the same person.

Implicit population

Quoting a rate without ever stating the denominator population — leaving the reader unable to judge whether the figure is even meaningful.

Your weekly quiz and Final Presentation Project will ask you to identify the case definition and population at risk implied by a published abstract — just like the examples above.
