MODULE 2 · TOPIC 4 · STATISTICS FOR EPIDEMIOLOGY

Principles of Statistical Inference and Hypothesis Testing

From a Sample of Patients to a Statement About All Patients — Study Notes

Every time a paper reports "OR 2.80, 95% CI 1.64–4.97" or "p < 0.05", it is making a promise about patients it never measured. Statistical inference is the machinery that lets a study of 344 diabetic patients in Harare, or 201 hypertensive patients in Mazowe, say something trustworthy about patients everywhere. These notes build that machinery from plain language upward, using published Zimbabwean and Southern African evidence throughout. Section 01 · Foundations

Why Statistical Inference Matters to a Clinician

No study ever measures every patient who has, or will have, a condition. A researcher in Harare cannot test every diabetic patient in Zimbabwe, and a trial in Beitbridge cannot follow every person at risk of malaria. Instead, researchers measure a sample and use statistics to make an honest, quantified guess about everyone else — the population. That leap, from sample to population, is called statistical inference, and it is what turns a table of numbers into a claim you can trust (or challenge).

344

Diabetic patients enrolled in a Harare outpatient study of nephropathy — a sample used to estimate nephropathy risk for diabetic patients across Zimbabwe more broadly (Machingura et al., 2017, Am J Trop Med Hyg).

2,414

Individuals sampled across Beitbridge and Lupane districts to estimate malaria severity risk factors for the wider population living in Zimbabwe's malaria-elimination districts (Betera et al., 2024, Int J Environ Res Public Health).

Every time you read "OR 2.80 (95% CI 1.64–4.97)" or "p = 0.008", the authors are reporting the result of statistical inference — and this topic teaches you to read those numbers correctly instead of taking them on faith.

Section 02 · The Basic Vocabulary

Population vs Sample, Parameter vs Statistic

Before any test or calculation, get two pairs of words straight — almost every mistake in interpreting statistics traces back to blurring these.

POP

Population

Every person (or unit) we ultimately want to know about — for example, all diabetic patients attending public clinics in Harare. We almost never measure the whole population directly. Its true, unknown value is called a parameter.

SAMP

Sample

The smaller group we actually measured — for example, the 344 patients enrolled in the Harare nephropathy study. The value we calculate from it is called a statistic, and we use it to estimate the population parameter.

The symbols below look intimidating in a paper's methods section, but each one simply tells you whether a number describes the whole population (Greek letter) or the sample we actually measured (Latin letter):

QuantityPopulation (unknown, Greek)Sample (measured, Latin)
Mean (average)μ (mu)x̄ ("x-bar")
Proportionpp̄ ("p-hat")
Standard deviationσ (sigma)s
Sample sizeN (whole population)n (sample)
WHY "SAMPLE" IS NOT "POPULATION"

The TENART cohort at Newlands Clinic (Shamu et al., 2017, PLoS ONE) followed 605 patients who started ART between 2004 and 2006 for over 10 years. Their virological suppression rate of 90.3% is a sample statistic. It is a very good estimate of the suppression rate among similar long-term ART patients more broadly — but it is not automatically true for a newly diagnosed 19-year-old starting ART in 2026, or for a rural clinic with different adherence support. Every statistic comes with an implicit label: "estimated from this sample, under these conditions."

Read every clinical statistic with the question: what was the sample, and how far can I really generalise it?

Section 03 · Why Numbers Wobble

Sampling Variability and the Standard Error

Imagine you could repeat the Harare nephropathy study a thousand times, drawing a fresh sample of 344 patients from the same population each time. You would not get exactly 44.8% nephropathy prevalence every time — you might get 42%, or 47%, purely because different patients happened to be selected. This natural wobble from sample to sample is called sampling variability, and it is the reason we need the whole machinery of hypothesis testing in the first place.

SE

Standard Error (SE) — in plain language

The standard error measures how much a sample statistic (like x̄) would typically wobble from one sample to the next, if we could repeat the study many times. Think of it as: "how far off might our one sample be, just by bad luck?"

Two things shrink the SE — and therefore make our estimate more trustworthy:

  • A bigger sample (n): more patients means less room for chance to swing the average.
  • Less variability in the measurement itself: a tightly clustered outcome gives a more stable average than a widely scattered one.

A useful mental picture: throwing 5 darts gives a rougher idea of your "true" aim than throwing 500 darts. The standard error is a measure of that roughness.

SAMPLE SIZE AND PRECISION — ZIMPHIA vs A SMALL PILOT CLINIC

The 2020 Zimbabwe Population-Based HIV Impact Assessment (ZIMPHIA, Gonese et al.) tested tens of thousands of adults nationally and produced an HIV prevalence estimate of 12.9% with a tight confidence interval. A small pilot clinic testing 40 patients might find a similar 12–13% prevalence, but its confidence interval would be far wider — the same true population value, estimated with far less precision, purely because n is smaller.

This is exactly why very small studies can report "no significant difference" even when a real difference exists in the population — their standard errors are simply too large to detect it confidently. We return to this as a Type II error later in this topic.

Section 04 · Framing the Question

The Null Hypothesis (H0) and the Alternative Hypothesis (H1)

Every hypothesis test starts by writing down two competing, precise statements about the population — before looking at the result. Think of it like a courtroom: the accused (H0) is presumed "not guilty" (no effect) until the evidence is strong enough to overturn that presumption.

H0

Null hypothesis

There is no true difference or no true association in the population. Any difference we see in our sample is assumed, until proven otherwise, to be due to chance (sampling variability) alone.

H1

Alternative hypothesis

There is a true difference or association in the population — the thing the researcher usually suspects and is trying to demonstrate.

A hypothesis test never "proves H1 true". It only asks: is our sample data unusual enough, if H0 were actually correct, that we should abandon H0? This is a subtle but crucial point we return to when discussing the p-value.

Clinical questionH0H1
Does retinopathy predict nephropathy in diabetic patients in Harare?Retinopathy is not associated with nephropathy (true OR = 1)Retinopathy is associated with nephropathy (true OR ≠ 1)
Do adults have a different risk of severe malaria than children in Beitbridge/Lupane?Risk of severe malaria is the same in adults and childrenRisk of severe malaria differs between adults and children
Did the mobile-van TB case-finding strategy detect a different number of cases than door-to-door visits (DETECTB trial)?No difference in cases detected between strategiesA difference exists between strategies
A COMMON CONFUSION

Students sometimes write H0 as "there is no important difference." This is wrong. H0 is a precise mathematical statement — usually "the true difference, ratio, or association equals zero (or one, for a ratio)" — not a judgement about clinical importance. Whether a real, non-zero effect is clinically important is a separate question, covered later in this topic under "statistical vs clinical significance".

Keep the mathematical claim (what the test can address) separate from the value judgement (what a clinician must still decide).

Section 05 · The Recipe

The Five-Step Hypothesis Testing Framework

Every hypothesis test — whatever the specific formula — follows the same five steps. You will meet the specific tests (t-test, chi-square) in the next topic; here we fix the logic that all of them share.

1
State H0 & H1
2
Set α (e.g. 0.05)
3
Choose & run the test
4
Get the p-value
5
Decide & interpret

1 · State H0 and H1

Write both hypotheses down precisely, before analysing the data. Doing this after seeing the result invites bias.

2 · Choose a significance level (α)

Almost always α = 0.05 (5%) in clinical research — the maximum acceptable risk of a false-positive conclusion, agreed before looking at the results.

3 · Choose and run the appropriate test

The choice (t-test, chi-square, and so on) depends on the type of data and the study design — the subject of Module 2, Topic 5.

4 · Obtain the test statistic and p-value

The test converts the observed data into a single number (the test statistic), which is then converted into a p-value.

5 · Decide and interpret

If p < α, reject H0 ("statistically significant"). If p ≥ α, fail to reject H0 ("not statistically significant") — which is not the same as proving H0 true.

MUNGATI ET AL. 2014, BMC CARDIOVASCULAR DISORDERS — MAZOWE DISTRICT

Investigating hypertension care at three Mazowe District hospitals, the authors asked whether missing multiple types of antihypertensive drugs was associated with poor compliance. H0: number of drug types is not associated with missed doses. α = 0.05. Test: chi-square. Result: chi-square = 0.85, P = 0.65. Decision: since 0.65 ≥ 0.05, fail to reject H0 — no statistically significant association detected in this sample.

The same paper found a very different result for complications: patients with poorly controlled hypertension had significantly higher odds of complications than those with controlled hypertension (POR 7.71, 95% CI 1.78–33.4) — here p was small enough to reject H0.

Section 06 · The Most Misunderstood Number in Medicine

The P-value, Explained Without the Jargon

If you remember only one definition from this topic, make it this one — because the p-value is quoted constantly and misunderstood almost as often.

DEF

The p-value

The probability of getting a result at least as extreme as the one we observed in our sample, if the null hypothesis (H0) were actually true — that is, if there really was no difference or no association in the population.

In plain words: "How surprising is our result, assuming nothing interesting is really going on?"

A small p-value (conventionally < 0.05) means our result would be unusual under H0 — so we doubt H0 and lean toward H1. It is a statement about the data, given an assumption about the population — not a statement about the population directly.

Two things a p-value does not tell you, however often people treat it as if it did:

It is not the probability that H0 is true

p = 0.03 does not mean "there is a 3% chance of no real effect." It is a statement about how surprising the data would be under H0, not the probability of H0 itself.

It does not measure the size or importance of an effect

A tiny, clinically trivial difference can still produce a very small p-value if the sample is huge enough — and a large, important difference can produce a large p-value if the sample is small (see "sampling variability" above).

MACHINGURA ET AL. 2017, AM J TROP MED HYG — HARARE NEPHROPATHY STUDY

Comparing severe (severely increased albuminuria) nephropathy between type 1 and type 2 diabetes patients, the authors reported p = 0.866. Correct reading: if there truly was no difference between type 1 and type 2 patients in the population, a difference at least this large would happen 86.6% of the time by chance alone — so this sample gives no good reason to doubt H0. Incorrect reading: "there is an 86.6% chance the two groups are the same" — the p-value cannot tell us that.

Contrast this with the same paper's finding for retinopathy: OR = 2.80 (95% CI: 1.64–4.97), a result unlikely under H0 — the association was retained as statistically significant.

α

Significance level. The maximum Type I error rate we are willing to accept, chosen before the study (usually 5%). It directly sets the threshold p-value must beat.

1−β

Power. The probability of correctly detecting a real effect if one truly exists. Power increases with a bigger sample size, a larger true effect, and less measurement variability — the same three factors that shrink the standard error.

SMALL SAMPLES, MISSED SIGNALS

Many pilot studies and district-level surveys across Southern Africa enrol only 100–200 participants, largely for practical and budget reasons. With small n, the standard error is large, confidence intervals are wide, and true but modest effects can easily fail to reach p < 0.05 — a Type II error. The 23 health workers interviewed in the Mazowe hypertension study (Mungati et al., 2014), for instance, is too small a group to detect anything but a very large difference in knowledge between subgroups.

A non-significant result from a small study should prompt the question "was this study big enough to find an effect if one existed?" — not an automatic conclusion that no effect exists.

Section 08 · The Practical Partner to the P-value

Confidence Intervals: A Range You Can Trust

A p-value only gives a yes/no verdict. A confidence interval (CI) gives a range of plausible values for the true population parameter — and clinicians usually learn far more from the range than from the verdict alone.

95% CI

The 95% confidence interval, in plain language

If we repeated the study 100 times, drawing a fresh sample each time and calculating a new 95% CI each time, about 95 of those 100 intervals would contain the true population value. Practically, we treat our one calculated interval as a plausible range for the truth — wider intervals mean less precise information; narrower intervals mean more precise information.

A simple shortcut links a CI directly back to statistical significance, without needing to check a p-value at all:

Ratio measures (OR, RR, HR)

"No effect" = 1. If the 95% CI does not cross 1 (e.g. 1.64–4.97), the result is statistically significant at roughly the 5% level.

Difference measures (mean difference, risk difference)

"No effect" = 0. If the 95% CI does not cross 0, the result is statistically significant at roughly the 5% level.

READING THE NUMBER LINE — THE "NO EFFECT" VALUE IS 1

The dashed line marks "no effect" (OR/RR = 1). The green and red intervals sit entirely to the right of the line (significant); the orange interval straddles it (not significant). Notice the red interval is also much wider than the green one — a real, significant effect, but estimated with less precision, likely reflecting a smaller number of complication events.

Section 09 · A Quick Distinction

One-Tailed vs Two-Tailed Tests

This distinction is about which direction of difference H1 allows for — and it must be decided before the data are analysed, never afterward.

2-T

Two-tailed test

H1 allows the effect to go in either direction ("different from", not "bigger than" or "smaller than"). This is the default and almost always the correct, more conservative choice in clinical research.

1-T

One-tailed test

H1 specifies a single direction only (e.g. "the new drug lowers blood pressure", not "changes" it). Rarely justified, because it assumes in advance that an effect in the "wrong" direction is impossible or uninteresting — a strong assumption clinicians should be very cautious about.

A practical rule for reading papers: unless the authors give a strong, pre-specified biological reason for a one-tailed test, treat a two-tailed test as the expected default — and be sceptical of a one-tailed test chosen only after the direction of the result was already known.

Section 10 · The Question That Matters Most

Statistical Significance vs Clinical Significance

A small p-value answers "is this probably not just chance?" It does not answer "does this matter for my patient?" Confusing the two is one of the most common — and most consequential — errors in reading medical literature.

Statistical significance

p < α (usually 0.05), or a 95% CI that excludes "no effect". Tells you the result is unlikely to be pure chance. Strongly influenced by sample size — a huge sample can make a trivial effect statistically significant.

Clinical significance

Whether the size of the effect is large enough to change patient care, prognosis or policy. A matter of clinical judgement, informed by the point estimate and confidence interval — not decided by the p-value alone.

SIGNIFICANT BUT WEAK, vs SIGNIFICANT AND STRONG

Statistically significant, small effect: in the Harare nephropathy study, higher fructosamine was a statistically significant predictor of nephropathy, but with OR = 1.00 (95% CI 1.00–1.01) per unit — a real association, but each single-unit change carries almost no individual clinical weight (Machingura et al., 2017).

Statistically significant, large effect: in the same broad literature, retinopathy carried OR = 2.80 (95% CI 1.64–4.97) for nephropathy — both statistically significant and clinically substantial, making retinopathy a genuinely useful red flag for nephropathy screening in diabetic patients.

Two results can both be "p < 0.05" and still mean very different things for how a clinician should act. Always look at the size of the effect (the point estimate) and its precision (the CI width), not just whether p crossed 0.05.

Section 11 · Reading Papers Critically

Common Pitfalls When Interpreting Inference

Keep these four habits of mind whenever you read a "p =" or "95% CI" in a clinical paper.

"Trending toward significance"

p = 0.07 or p = 0.09 is not "almost significant" — it is simply not statistically significant at the pre-set α. The threshold was chosen in advance precisely to avoid this kind of after-the-fact softening.

Multiple testing inflates false positives

Testing 20 different associations at α = 0.05 each means roughly one is expected to be "significant" purely by chance, even if nothing real is happening. Watch for studies that ran many comparisons but report only the significant ones.

Absence of evidence ≠ evidence of absence

A non-significant result in a small study (like the Mazowe drug-count comparison, P = 0.65) does not prove there is no true association — it may simply reflect low power (a Type II error risk).

Association is not causation

Even a tight, statistically significant confidence interval (e.g. retinopathy and nephropathy, OR 2.80) shows a real association — it does not by itself prove retinopathy causes nephropathy; both may share a common underlying driver, such as duration or severity of diabetes.
