Comparing Means and Proportions

t-tests and Chi-square Tests, Explained From Scratch — Study Notes

Topic 4 gave you the logic of hypothesis testing — H0, H1, p-values, confidence intervals. This topic hands you the two actual tools clinicians reach for most often: the t-test, for comparing averages, and the chi-square test, for comparing proportions and categories. Section 01 · From logic to tools

Why This Topic Matters

Topic 4 taught you the courtroom logic of a hypothesis test: assume H0 (no difference), collect evidence, and see if that evidence is surprising enough to abandon H0. But it never told you exactly how to calculate that evidence for a specific kind of question. That is what this topic does. Almost every comparison a clinician makes falls into one of two buckets — comparing an average (mean age, mean CD4 count, mean weight) or comparing a proportion (percentage male, percentage with a complication, percentage suppressed) — and each bucket has its own tool.

By the end of this topic you will be able to look at a results table in a journal article, recognise which of these two tools produced each number, and judge whether the authors used it correctly.

Section 02 · A 60-second refresher

Data Types, Quick Recap

Choosing the right test always starts with one question: what kind of outcome am I comparing? There are really only two broad answers.

NUM

Continuous (numeric) data

A measurement that can take many possible values along a scale — age in years, CD4 count, weight in kilograms, HbA1c. We summarise it with a mean (average). Comparing means between groups is a job for a t-test.

CAT

Categorical data

Data that falls into named groups — sex, diabetes type, HIV status, cured/not cured. We summarise it with a proportion or percentage. Comparing proportions between groups is a job for a chi-square test.

A simple test: if you can sensibly calculate an average of it, it is continuous. If you can only count how many people fall into each named box, it is categorical.

Section 03 · The decision every analysis starts with

Choosing the Right Test

Once you know your outcome type and your study design, the choice of test almost makes itself. This table is the single most useful reference in this whole topic — keep it close.

Outcome typeWhat you are comparingTest
Continuous (e.g. age, CD4 count)One sample's mean vs a known/target valueOne-sample t-test
ContinuousMeans of two different, independent groupsIndependent (unpaired) t-test
ContinuousThe same people, measured twicePaired t-test
Categorical (e.g. sex, outcome status)Proportions across two or more independent groupsChi-square test
Categorical, small expected countsSame as above, but a small sampleFisher's exact test
WHY PAIRED IS DIFFERENT

A Zimbabwean study of weight and blood pressure trends in people starting dolutegravir-, efavirenz-, or atazanavir-based ART at Newlands Clinic tracked the same patients before and after starting their regimen, observing median weight gains of roughly 4–5 kg over 24 months (Shamu et al., 2024, J Int AIDS Soc). Whenever the same person contributes two measurements, an independent t-test would be the wrong tool — it assumes the two groups are made of different people. The right logic is a paired comparison, which we build in Section 07.

Rule of thumb: same people twice → think "paired". Different people, one measurement each → think "independent".

Section 04 · Three tools, one family

The t-test Family

Every t-test asks a version of the same question: "is the difference we see between averages bigger than we would expect from chance alone?" They differ only in what is being compared.

1 · One-sample t-test

Compares the mean of a single sample against a known or expected value. Example: is the mean systolic blood pressure of a clinic's hypertensive patients different from the national treatment target of 140 mmHg?

2 · Independent (unpaired) t-test

Compares the means of two separate groups of different people. Example: is mean age different between men and women screened for diabetic foot disease in Harare?

3 · Paired t-test

Compares two measurements taken on the same people at two different times, or under two different conditions. Example: is a patient's mean weight different 24 months after starting a new ART regimen, compared with before?

When you have more than two groups to compare (for example, three ART regimens), a t-test is no longer enough — that calls for a different tool called ANOVA, which sits outside this topic but builds on exactly the same logic.

Section 05 · What the number is actually doing

Inside the t-test: Signal Over Noise

You never need to calculate a t-statistic by hand — software does that. But understanding the idea behind the formula will make every t-test result you ever read make sense.

t

The t-statistic, in one sentence

t = the difference between the two averages ÷ how much that difference could wobble by chance (the standard error, from Topic 4).

In other words: t compares the signal (how far apart the two averages really are) against the noise (how much sampling variability alone could move them). A big t means the signal is loud compared with the noise — a real difference is more believable.

Two things make t bigger: a larger gap between the averages, or a smaller standard error (from bigger samples or less noisy data — see Topic 4, Section 3).

Section 06 · Reading a real comparison of means

Worked Example: Comparing Two Independent Means

A Harare study screened 352 people living with diabetes for diabetic foot disease across 16 primary care clinics. Among many other questions, the researchers asked a simple one: is the average age of the men in the sample different from the average age of the women?

H0

Null hypothesis

The true mean age is the same for men and women in the population this sample represents.

H1

Alternative hypothesis

The true mean age differs between men and women.

The combined sample had a mean age of 57.9 years (SD 14). When the researchers compared men and women directly, age was "comparable by gender (p > 0.05)" (Kuguyo et al., 2024, BMC Public Health) — exactly the kind of question an independent-samples t-test is built to answer.

READING THE RESULT

Correct reading: the data gave no good evidence, in this sample, of a true age difference between men and women — we fail to reject H0. This does not prove the ages are identical; it simply means this comparison did not detect a difference.

Incorrect reading: "men and women are the same age" — a t-test can never prove two groups are identical, only that a sample failed to show a difference.

Compare this to the same paper's next finding in Section 10: when the outcome switched from a continuous variable (age) to a categorical one (diabetes type), the tool switched too — from a t-test question to a chi-square one.

Section 07 · The "same person, twice" idea

Worked Example: The Paired Idea in Practice

A Newlands Clinic study followed people living with HIV in Harare who started or switched to dolutegravir (DTG)-, efavirenz (EFV)-, or atazanavir (ATV/r)-based ART, measuring each patient's weight repeatedly before and after the change (Shamu et al., 2024, J Int AIDS Soc). At 24 months, estimated weight gains were around 4.5 kg in women and 3.7 kg in men on DTG — roughly twice the gain seen on efavirenz.

PAIR

Why this is a "paired" idea

Each patient supplies two linked measurements — their own weight before, and their own weight after. A paired t-test would work with the list of individual differences (after minus before) for each person, and ask whether the average difference is convincingly different from zero.

This study itself used more advanced statistical models built for repeated measurements over time, rather than a simple paired t-test — but the underlying logic, "does this outcome change within the same person over time", is the paired idea in its purest form.

Wrong way to test thisRight way to think about it
Independent t-test comparing "before" weights vs "after" weights as if they were two unrelated groupsPaired comparison: for each patient, calculate (after − before), then test whether the average of those differences is zero

Ignoring the pairing throws away information and usually makes it harder to detect a real change — because it fails to cancel out each person's own starting point.

Section 08 · The tool for proportions

The Chi-square Test

Once your outcome is categorical (a named group, not a number), the question becomes: "is the split between categories different across groups?" That is exactly what a chi-square (χ²) test of independence answers.

Q

The question it answers

Is there an association between two categorical variables — for example, between sex and diabetes type, or between number of drug types taken and whether doses are missed?

H0

Its null hypothesis

The two categorical variables are independent — knowing someone's category on one variable tells you nothing about their category on the other.

The data is arranged in a contingency table — rows for one variable, columns for the other, with the count of people in each combination in the cells.

Section 09 · What the number is actually doing

Inside Chi-square: Observed vs Expected

The chi-square statistic compares what you actually saw in your table (observed counts) with what you would expect to see if H0 were true and there really was no association (expected counts).

χ²

The chi-square statistic, in one sentence

For every cell in the table: take (observed − expected), square it (so gaps in either direction count the same and do not cancel out), divide by the expected count (a fairness correction so cells with small expected numbers are not swamped) — then add all the cells together.

The bigger the total gap between observed and expected, the bigger χ² is, and the less plausible H0 (independence) becomes.

Section 10 · Reading real chi-square results

Worked Examples: Chi-square in Practice

Here are two real, published chi-square results from Zimbabwe — one where H0 was not rejected, and one where it was.

Number of drug types vs missed doses (Mungati et al., 2014)

Among 201 hypertensive patients in Mazowe District, the researchers tested whether the number of different drug types a patient was taking was associated with whether doses were missed. Result: χ² = 0.85, P = 0.65.

Since P (0.65) is far above α (0.05), we fail to reject H0 — this dataset gave no statistically significant evidence that drug count and missed doses are associated. It does not prove they are unrelated; a Type II error remains possible.

Sex vs type of diabetes (Kuguyo et al., 2024)

In the same 352-person Harare diabetic foot study, the distribution of diabetes type (type 1 vs type 2) "significantly differed by gender (p < 0.001)" — roughly 26% of men had type 1 diabetes, compared with about 15% of women.

Here P is far below α (0.05), so H0 (no association between sex and diabetes type) is rejected. This is exactly the categorical, proportion-based question a chi-square test exists to answer — contrast this with the continuous, mean-based age comparison in Section 06, from the very same dataset.

Section 11 · A number every printout shows

Degrees of Freedom, in Plain Terms

Every t-test and chi-square printout reports "df". You do not need to calculate it, but it helps to know what it means: how many values in your calculation were free to vary once the totals were fixed.

t

For t-tests

Roughly, df = total number of people − number of group means being estimated. An independent t-test comparing two groups of sizes n1 and n2 has df = n1 + n2 − 2. More people → more df → a more precise, trustworthy test.

χ²

For chi-square tests

df = (number of rows − 1) × (number of columns − 1). A simple 2×2 table (like sex vs diabetes type) has df = (2−1)×(2−1) = 1.

Why it matters: df determines exactly which reference distribution (t-distribution or chi-square distribution) is used to turn your statistic into a p-value. Software does this automatically — your job is only to notice whether df looks sensible for your sample size and table shape.

Section 12 · When these tools do not fit

Assumptions and What to Use Instead

Every statistical test comes with fine print. Knowing the main assumptions lets you spot when a paper (or your own analysis) has reached for the wrong tool.

t-tests assume roughly normal data

The outcome should be roughly bell-shaped, especially in small samples (large samples are more forgiving). Machingura et al. (2017) reported HbA1c as a median with interquartile range, not a mean with SD — a strong hint the authors judged it too skewed for a standard t-test.

Observations must be independent

Each person should contribute independently (unless you are deliberately using a paired design). Measuring the same patient's weight five times and treating all five as separate people would break this assumption.

Chi-square needs enough expected counts

As a rule of thumb, every cell in the contingency table should have an expected count of at least 5. Below that, results become unreliable and Fisher's exact test is the safer choice — common in small Southern African pilot studies.

When normality fails, go non-parametric

Skewed continuous data can be compared with the Mann–Whitney U test (instead of an independent t-test) or the Wilcoxon signed-rank test (instead of a paired t-test) — both compare ranks rather than means, and need no normality assumption.

Section 13 · A quick-reference flow

Quick Decision Flow

Ask yourself these questions, in order, next time you need to choose a test.

Is my outcome continuous (a number, like age or CD4 count) or categorical (a named group)?
→
Continuous → t-test family
Categorical → chi-square
If continuous: am I comparing the same people twice, or two different groups?
→
Same people → paired t-test
Different groups → independent t-test
Does my continuous data look roughly bell-shaped, or is it badly skewed with a small sample?
→
Roughly normal → t-test
Badly skewed → Mann–Whitney / Wilcoxon
If categorical: does every cell in my table have an expected count of 5 or more?
→
Yes → chi-square test
No → Fisher's exact test
Section 14 · Reading papers critically

Common Pitfalls

Keep these habits of mind whenever you meet a t-test or chi-square result in a clinical paper.

Using an independent t-test on paired data

Treating "before" and "after" measurements on the same patients as two separate, unrelated groups wastes information and usually understates the real effect.

Running chi-square with tiny expected counts

A 2×2 table with only a handful of people in one cell needs Fisher's exact test, not a standard chi-square test, or the p-value cannot be trusted.

Forgetting that "not significant" is not "no difference"

The Mazowe drug-count comparison (P = 0.65) shows no significant association was detected — it is not proof that none exists, especially with a modest sample size.

Ignoring effect size

A t-test or chi-square p-value tells you whether a difference is likely real; it says nothing about whether it is clinically large. Always look at the actual means, proportions, or effect estimates alongside the p-value.
